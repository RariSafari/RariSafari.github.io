Lead

Lead, which they called “plumbum”, was widely used in Roman times and has been around for thousands of years. They used lead to make pipes, writing tablets, and coins. They also used it to make pots and pans, which later caused lead poisoning.

It is a natural element, but it is rarely found as a pure element. Instead it is most commonly found in ores of lead sulfide (galena). It is isolated by being smelted which converts the ore to the compound of lead oxide. The lead oxide is then heated with pure carbon which isolates the lead. The formula looks like this:

PbO2 + C--heated→CO2 + Pb

Lead’s boiling point is approximately 1750°C, its melting point is 372.46°C, its density is 11.34 grams per cm3, and its hardness is marked at 1.5 Mohs. It is solid at room temperature, it is gray colored, it has 82 protons and 4 valence electrons.

Lead use was decreased when the effects of lead poisoning were understood. For example, it was used in ammunition, sheet lead (used in building construction), solder, piping for water and sewage, ball bearings, radiation shielding, and as a gasoline additive. Though lead is hardly a frequently used element, it is still used in lead stoage batteries.

No one is sure how lead got its English name, but its chemical symbol comes from its Roman name. Pure lead is priced at $2.45 for 100 grams, $11 a pound. It can cause immediate and long-term health problems, especially with children.
